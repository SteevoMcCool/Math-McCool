Definition Of Limits

A limit of f(x) as x approaches a value descibes the behavior of the function around that value.

In other words, a limit represents the y-value based on the function's curve, not the corresponding x-value.

Example of Limits

Let's suppose we define f(x) = (x-2)2 x-2 . If we were to try and calculate f(2) we would get an error.

This is because the denominator of f(2) is 2-2, which is zero. Since we cannot divide by zero, the f(2) is undefined. On the graph, there is a hole at x=2.

However, using any of 3 methods, we can still find lim f(x) x->2

Solving Limits Numerically (GC required)

Let's suppose we have the previous example of f(x) and would like to solve it numerically. This means we are going to use a table of values to determine the limit.

To get an accurate table of values on a graphing calculator, first enter the function in by pressing Y1 and typing the function into your calculator. Remember: Calculators follow pemdas, so parenthesis are important. Your function entry should look like Y1=(x-2)2/(x-2) .

Once done, you need to access the calculator's table settings. To do this, press 2nd + window (located next to the Y1 button). Leave the bottom two options on Auto

After that, change TblStart to the value that X is approaching. In this case, set TblStart=2.Change the second option (Delta Table) to a relatively small number, <=0.01.


Once you have your table set up, click 2nd + graph. You should see a table similar to the table on the right. Scroll upwards until 2 is in the middle.

As you can see, as the x-values get closer to 2, the y-values get closer to 0.

This means that we can conclude that lim f(x) = 0 x->2

Remember, the limit is determined by the behavoir around the point. So even though the point is undefined, there is still a limit at that point.


In my personal opinion, this is the best way to calculate limits. It is fast, simple, and removes any major chance of human error. However, you won't have a graphing calculator for all parts of the AP exam, so it is important to learn the other ways to find limits.

Solving Limits Algebraically

Let's define f(x) = x2+5x+6 x+3 and attempt to find lim f(x) x->-3

Your first step is to check if direct substiution will work by plugging -3 into the denominator. When you do this, you get -3 + 3 = 0, which is a divide by zero error. Direct substiution won't work yet.

The next step is to check if x-3 is a factor of the numerator. To do this, you can factor the numerator using quadratic factorting, which results in (x+2)(x+3). Your function now looks like f(x) = (x+2)(x+3) x+3

You can then cancel the x+3 in the numerator with the x+3 in the denominator. This looks like lim (x+2)(x+3) x+3 x->-3 = lim x+2 x-> -3

Now you can directly substitute -3 into x+2 --> -3+2 --> -1

lim f(x) = -1 x->-3

Squeeze Theorem, Reciprocal Theorem

The Squeeze/Sandwhich Theorem

This Theorem attempts to find the limit of a function by "Sandwhiching" or squeezing it between 2 other functions.

lim x2 * sin(1/x) x -> 0

Since direct substitiotion won't work, we can Sandwhich this function between 2 functions. We know that sin() of anything is always inbetween -1 and 1, so we can say:

-1 ≤ sin(1/x) ≤ 1

Now, we can multiply the entire inequality by x2 to get: -x2 ≤ x2 * sin(1/x) ≤ x2 Now let's take the limit to zero of all these:

lim -x2 x->0 ≤ lim x2 * sin(1/x) x->0 ≤ lim x2x->0   =   0 ≤ lim x2 * sin(1/x) x->0 ≤ 0 Then boom! We know the answer is inbetween zero and zero, so we know the answer is zero!

The Reciprocal Theorem

This Theorem attempts to find the limit of a f(x) to zero by taking the limit of the f(1/x) to infinity

lim x2 * sin(1/x) x -> 0

Since direct substitiotion won't work, we can Recipricol this function. When we do that, we get 1/x2 * sin(x)

Now we need to find lim 1/x2 * sin(x) x -> ∞

Then very easily, we know that the end behavior rules will follow 1/x2. This is because as x-> ∞, x continuously gets bigger (meaning 1/x2 gets a lot smaller), whereas sin(x) is always inbetween -1 and 1. Therefore, we know this limit is the same as lim 1/x2 x -> ∞ , which is zero :). For more on end behavior and limits to infinity, click here

Types of Discontinuities

A continuous function is a function with no jumps, holes, or vertical asymtopes*. For example, y= ex is continuous, buy y= 1/x is not. You can prove continuity of f(x), x=c by stating lim f(x) x->c- = lim f(x) = f(c) x->c+

A removable discontinuity looks like a hole in the graph. Essentially, if you just shaded over that one point, the graph would be continuous. You can prove a removable discontinuity by stating lim f(x) x->c- = lim f(x) x->c+ , and lim f(x) x->c- ≠ f(c)

A jump discontinuity looks like a gap between 2 points (refer to this function). You can prove a jump discontinuity by stating the left and right handed limits arent equal, but both limits exist.

You can prove an INFINITE DISCONTINUITY by proving at least one of the one-sided limits goes to infinity. This is also called a VERTICAL ASYMPTOPE

You won't recieve an oscilatting discontinuity, but in most cases, it follows the behavior of a removable discontinuity inside of a wavy function.

Properties Of Limits

The properities of limits say that, for any limit, you can separate any operation and take the limit of both parts. For example:

lim f(x)*g(x) x->c = lim f(x) x->c * lim g(x) x->c ||     ||lim f(x) - g(x) x->c = lim f(x) x->c - lim g(x) x->c

These rules apply for all operations, not just addition and subtraction. We can use these rules to solve limits of special values.

Let's attempt to solve lim sin 4x x + 1 x->0

Using the properties of limits, we know lim sin 4x x + 1 x->0 = lim sin 4x x x->0 + lim 1 x->0 . Since we know that the limit of a constant is the constant, we can rewrite this as 1 + lim sin 4x x x->0

Let's multiply the limit by 4/4, aka one. When we do this, we get 1 + lim 4 * sin 4x 4x x->0 , which we can now rewrite as 1 + lim sin 4x x x->0 . Using properties of limits, we can separate this into, 1 + lim 4 x -> 0 * lim sin 4x x x->0 .

By remembering the table to the right, we can recognize that lim sin 4x x x->0 = 1, as it follows the Limits to Zero With Sine rule.

After all simplification, we get 1 + 4* 1, which is 5

lim sin(cx) cx = 1 x->0

Note that for this to work, the c in the numerator must equal the c in the denominator. Here is an example:

lim sin(6x) 6x = 1 x->0   lim sin(4x) 3x = ?? x->0

lim cos(cx) ax DNE x->0

For cosine, the c in the numerator does not have to equal the c in the denominator. Here is an example:

Both limits don't exist.

Limits to Infinity/ End Behavior Model

You should know the basic shapes of graphs and functions. For exapmle, f(x)=x2 - x + 1 is a parabola. But what if we did: lim x2 - x + 1 x->∞ . To do this, we use an end behavior model (EBM).

An EBM is a shorthand way to describe how a function behaves as it gets closer and closer to infinity. The EBM of all polynomial functions is the term with the highest degree.

An EBM can be for only the left (LEBM) or the right (REBM) of a function. To prove E(x) is a LEBM of f(x), you must prove lim f(x) E(x) = 1 x->-∞, and to prove REBM: lim f(x) E(x) = 1 x->+∞

If either EBM is a constant (ie LEBM of f(X)=4), then that constant is a Horizontal Asymtope of that function.

Let's do 2 examples. First, let's return to our original function, f(x)=x2 - x + 1. We know the EBM of this function is E(x)=x2, but now, let's prove it. To do this, we must keep in mind the shape of y= 1 x. For this proof, we need to do lim x2 - x + 1 x2> x-> ∓ ∞. Using alegbra, this limit is equal to lim x2 x2 - x x2 + 2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞. Using properties of limits, we can break this down even further, into: lim x2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ - lim x x2 x-> ∓ ∞ + lim 2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ .

Now we can solve each limit individually. First we know lim x2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ = 1 , because the numerator and the denominator reduce completely to one. Secondly, you can cancel an x from the numerator and the denominator. lim x x * x x-> ∓ ∞ = lim 1 x x-> ∓ ∞ . If you remember the shape of this common function, you'd know this limit to +∞ or -∞ is zero (therefore there is a horizontal asymtope for 1/x at y=0). Finally, we have: lim 2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ . We also know this graph has a horizontal asymtope at x=0, so this limit is also zero,

After solving all those limits, we did lim x2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ - lim x x2 x-> ∓ ∞ + lim 2 x2 x-> ∓ ∞ = 1 - 0 + 0 = 1. . Since this limit is 1, we know E(x)=x2 is the LEBM, REBM, and EBM of f(x)=x2 - x + 1

For the second example, we will find the EBM of x2 + 3x. AH, we have a PROBLEMO! One of the exponents is a variable, so how would we know which term has the higher degree. Well, we know that when x < 2, the exponent of 3x is also going to be less than 2. Which means that since as x goes to the left, where all numbers are < 2 , we know that EL(x)= x2 is the LEMB. This also means we know that ER(x)= 3x is the REBM.

Shortcuts with horizontal asymtopes

Use the EBM (aka highest degree) in the numerator and denominator to determine the horizontal asymtope.

High-Degree Denominator

When the highest degree in your denominator is greater than the highest degree in your numerator, the horizontal asymtope is always x=0

f(x) = x3 + 2x4 -x2 + 5x - 2 -8x5 + 4

Since 5 > 4, the highest degree of the denominator is greater than the highest degree of the numerator, so f(x) has a horizontal asymtope at x=0

Even-Degree Fraction

When the highest degree in your denominator equals the highest degree in your numerator, the horizontal asymtope is their coefficents divided by eachother

f(x) = x3 + 2x4 -x2 + 5x - 2 -8x4 + 4

Since 4 = 4, we need to divide 2-8 to get a horizontal asymptope of f(x) at x=-1/4

High-Degree Numerator

When the highest degree in your denominator is less than the highest degree in your numerator, there is no horizontal asymtope

f(x) = x3 + 2x4 -x2 + 5x - 2 -8x3 + 4     g(x) = 4x

Since 3 < 4, the highest degree of the denominator is less than the highest degree of the numerator, so f(x) no horizontal asymptope. For similar reasons, g(x) has no horizontal asymptope.

Limit of a function within a function

Let's say that f(x) = h(g(x)) and we want to find lim f(x). x->-3

If h and g are both continuous functions, this is very simple. You can just plug in 3 to find lim f(x) = h(g(3)). x->-3

However, if h and g are not continuous at x=3, you have to find lim h(x), which is a little more complicated. x->lim g(x)

First, you have to find the left- and right-handed limits of g(x) at 3. Then, you look at whether g(x) is increasing or decreasing on each side. Let's say that the right-handed limit ends up being a and the left-handed being b.

Then, you look at either the right- or left-handed limit of h(x) as x->a. If g(x) was decreasing on the right side, then you find the left-handed limit, and if it was increasing you find the right. Now do the same thing, but instead of the limit as x->a, find the limit as x->b. Remember that now we're looking on the left side of g(x) to determine whether or not it's increasing.

Finally, compare the two limits you got in the previous step. If they're the same value, your limit exits and you just found it! Otherwise, we can say that lim f(x) DNE. x->-3
